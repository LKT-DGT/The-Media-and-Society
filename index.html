The Media &
Its Role in Society

How do we receive information? Who controls the narrative? And how does media shape our understanding of the world — and of ourselves?

Why Media Matters

We live in an age of unprecedented media abundance. Every day, billions of people consume news, entertainment, and opinion through an ever-expanding range of platforms — from century-old newspapers to AI-generated video feeds. Media does not simply reflect the world; it actively shapes it.

The way stories are selected, framed, and distributed determines which voices are heard, which events are considered important, and how social and political issues are understood. Understanding how media works — its structures, its economics, its power — is one of the most important critical skills of the 21st century.

This resource explores two interconnected dimensions: first, how the media landscape itself has transformed in the digital age; and second, how this transformation has affected the individual and society — from access to information, to entertainment, to manipulation and disinformation.

Fact-Checking Lab

Phase 1: Tools · Phase 2: Method · Phase 3: Practice

Being able to critically verify information is one of the most important media literacy skills of the 21st century. Work through the tool overview and method guide, then investigate a claim using the report template.

Phase 1 — Your Fact-Checking Toolkit
🕵️ Snopes.com ↗
World's most well-known fact-checking site. Specialises in viral rumours, urban legends, and social media hoaxes.
Best for: viral claims, chain emails, conspiracy theories.
🏛️ FactCheck.org ↗
Run by the Annenberg Public Policy Center. Focuses on political claims — politicians' statements, election claims, and policy debates.
Best for: political speeches, election claims, government statistics.
📰 Reuters Fact Check ↗
Fact-checking by one of the world's most trusted wire agencies. Strong on international stories and health misinformation.
Best for: breaking news claims, health/science, international events.
🖼️ Google Reverse Image ↗
Upload any image to find where it first appeared online. Essential for detecting images used out of context.
Best for: checking if a photo is from a different event, year, or country.
🔎 TinEye ↗
Specialist reverse image search tracking where an image has appeared across the web with a precise timeline.
Best for: finding original image source and date, detecting manipulations.
🇬🇧 Full Fact (UK) ↗
UK's leading independent fact-checking organisation. Focuses on British politics, NHS, and media claims. Charity-funded.
Best for: UK political claims, NHS statistics, British tabloid headlines.
Phase 2 — Step-by-Step Method
1

Identify the Claim

What exactly is being claimed? Who made it, when, and in what context? Separate facts from opinions before you begin researching.

2

Check Fact-Checking Databases

Has it already been verified or debunked?

SnopesFactCheck.orgReutersFull Fact
3

Trace the Original Source

Follow citations back to the primary source. Reputable journalism cites verifiable sources. Be sceptical of anonymous or circular references.

4

Verify Images & Videos

Images are frequently taken out of context. Use reverse image search to check when and where an image was first published.

Google ImagesTinEye
5

Cross-Reference Reputable Outlets

Are established, independent news organisations also reporting this? If only partisan outlets cover it, treat it with caution.

6

Reach a Verdict

Assign: True, False, Misleading (technically accurate but deceptive), or Unverifiable. Document your reasoning clearly.

Key Terms Glossary

Topic 1 — The Changing Media Landscape
Traditional Media▸

Print (newspapers, magazines), radio, and television. Uses a one-to-many communication model — one broadcaster reaches a mass audience.

Examples: BBC, The Guardian, The Sun, radio stations.
Modern / Digital Media▸

Social media platforms, streaming services, podcasts, and online news portals. Uses a many-to-many model — anyone can publish.

Examples: TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Netflix, Spotify.
One-to-Many Model▸

A single sender communicates to a passive mass audience with no real-time interaction.

BBC evening news, a printed newspaper, a radio broadcast.
Many-to-Many Model▸

Multiple users communicate simultaneously — every user is both sender and receiver.

Twitter/X, TikTok comments, WhatsApp groups, Reddit threads.
Gatekeeping▸

The editorial process deciding which stories get published and how they are framed. Largely absent in digital media.

A newspaper editor choosing front-page stories vs. a tweet going viral with no editorial review.
Broadsheet▸

A quality newspaper prioritising in-depth, long-form journalism on politics, economics, and culture.

The Guardian, The Times, The Independent, The Daily Telegraph.
Tabloid▸

A newspaper prioritising entertainment, celebrity gossip, and sensationalism over investigative journalism.

The Sun, Daily Mail, Daily Mirror.
Public Broadcaster▸

A media institution funded by public money (licence fees or state subsidy), operating under a public service mandate.

BBC (UK), ARD/ZDF (Germany), ORF (Austria), ABC (Australia).
Commercial Media▸

Media funded by advertising revenue or subscriptions rather than public money.

Sky News, ITV, The Sun, most social media platforms.
Algorithms & Personalisation▸

Digital platforms use automated ranking systems to curate content based on your past behaviour, likes, and watch time.

YouTube's autoplay; TikTok's "For You Page"; Facebook's News Feed.
New Media Professions▸

The digital shift created entirely new careers: Content Creator, Influencer, Social Media Manager, SEO Expert, Podcast Producer, Data Journalist.

A YouTuber with 2M subscribers has greater daily reach than many regional newspapers.
Decline of Print Media▸

Print circulation has fallen sharply since the 2000s as advertising migrated online. Many regional newspapers have closed.

Over 250 UK local papers closed between 2005–2020.
Citizen Journalism▸

Non-professionals reporting on events via smartphones and social media in real time, without editorial oversight.

Eyewitness smartphone videos on X often break major stories before TV crews arrive.
UGC — User-Generated Content▸

Any content created and shared by ordinary users rather than professional media organisations.

TikTok videos, Instagram Reels, Reddit posts, YouTube vlogs, Wikipedia articles.
Media Convergence▸

The merging of previously separate media forms into integrated digital ecosystems. Traditional outlets now operate across print, web, video, podcast, and social media simultaneously.

The Guardian: started as a print newspaper, now has a website, YouTube channel, podcast network, and app.
Topic 2 — Impact on Individual & Society
Filter Bubble▸

Algorithms show you only content matching your existing interests — coined by Eli Pariser (2011). Your information universe shrinks without you noticing.

YouTube autoplay keeps reinforcing whatever you already watched. You never encounter the other side.
Echo Chamber▸

An environment where existing beliefs are reinforced by like-minded social interaction — and algorithmically. Leads to polarisation.

A Facebook group where any dissenting opinion is immediately removed or attacked.
Disinformation vs. Misinformation▸

Disinformation: deliberately false, spread with intent to deceive. Misinformation: false content spread without knowing it is false. Intent is the key distinction.

A fabricated BBC-style headline = disinformation. Sharing a debunked health claim you believed = misinformation.
Deepfakes▸

AI-generated synthetic media showing real people saying or doing things they never did. Increasingly realistic and difficult to detect.

A deepfake video of a politician "confessing" to corruption — circulated days before an election.
Framing▸

The way language, image selection, and emphasis shape how we perceive a story. The same event can be framed completely differently.

"Freedom fighter" vs. "terrorist" — same person, same actions, opposite frames and emotional responses.
Media Bias▸

One-sided or selective reporting — every outlet has some degree of bias based on ownership, audience, and ideology.

The Sun vs. The Guardian on the same immigration policy — same facts, radically different framing and tone.
Microtargeting▸

Using big data and psychological profiles to send personalised political messages to specific voter segments. Perfected by Cambridge Analytica using Facebook data in 2016.

Showing different Brexit messages to young urban voters vs. older rural voters based on their Facebook data.
Propaganda▸

Deliberate political manipulation through emotional appeals, slogans, repetition, and selective information. Social media has made propaganda faster, cheaper, and harder to detect.

State-sponsored troll farms posting thousands of messages to simulate grassroots public opinion (astroturfing).
Press Freedom▸

The right to publish without government interference — a cornerstone of democracy. Under growing pressure from political interference, economic crisis in journalism, and platform dominance.

Reporters Without Borders: Finland #1, Germany #10, USA #55, Russia #164 (2024).
Cyberbullying & Hate Speech▸

Cyberbullying: repeated online harassment, often anonymous. Hate speech: communication that dehumanises people based on identity.

Anonymous coordinated harassment campaigns driving journalists off social media entirely.

Key Terms Glossary
Traditional Media▸

Print (newspapers, magazines), radio, and television. Uses a one-to-many communication model — one broadcaster reaches a mass audience. Content is controlled by professional editorial gatekeepers with fixed publication cycles.

Examples: BBC (public broadcaster), The Guardian (broadsheet), The Sun (tabloid), radio stations.
Modern / Digital Media▸

Social media platforms, streaming services, podcasts, and online news portals. Uses a many-to-many model — anyone can publish. Content is personalised by algorithms and available 24/7 with real-time updates.

Examples: TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Netflix, Spotify, citizen journalism blogs.
One-to-Many Model▸

A single sender communicates to a passive mass audience with no real-time interaction — characteristic of traditional media like TV, radio, and print newspapers.

BBC evening news, a printed newspaper edition, a radio broadcast.
Many-to-Many Model▸

Multiple users communicate simultaneously — every user is both sender and receiver. This shift fundamentally changed media power dynamics.

Twitter/X discussions, TikTok comments, WhatsApp groups, Reddit threads.
Gatekeeping▸

The editorial process by which traditional media decides which stories get published and how they are framed. Largely absent in digital media.

A newspaper editor choosing front-page stories vs. a tweet going viral with no editorial review.
Broadsheet▸

A quality newspaper that prioritises in-depth, long-form journalism on politics, economics, and culture.

The Guardian, The Times, The Independent, The Daily Telegraph.
Tabloid▸

A newspaper that prioritises entertainment, celebrity gossip, and sensationalism over investigative journalism.

The Sun, Daily Mail, Daily Mirror.
Public Broadcaster▸

A media institution funded by public money (licence fees or state subsidy), operating under a public service mandate.

Commercial Media▸

Media funded by advertising revenue or subscriptions rather than public money.

Sky News, ITV, The Sun, most social media platforms and streaming services.
Algorithms & Personalisation▸

Digital platforms use automated ranking systems to curate content based on your past behaviour, likes, and watch time.

YouTube's autoplay; TikTok's "For You Page"; Facebook's News Feed.
New Media Professions▸

The digital shift created entirely new careers: Content Creator, Influencer, Social Media Manager, SEO Expert, Podcast Producer, Data Journalist.

Decline of Print Media▸

Print circulation has fallen sharply since the 2000s as advertising migrated online. Many regional newspapers have closed.

Citizen Journalism▸

Non-professionals reporting on events via smartphones and social media in real time, without formal training or editorial oversight.

Eyewitness smartphone videos on X (Twitter) often break major stories before TV crews arrive.
UGC — User-Generated Content▸

TikTok videos, Instagram Reels, Reddit posts, YouTube vlogs, Wikipedia articles.
